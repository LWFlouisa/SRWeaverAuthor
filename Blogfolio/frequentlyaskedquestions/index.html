Fine Art Focused Q And A

What painters are your main inspiration?

My two main inspirations are Jean Jacquez Walts ( an Alsatian Lithographer ), and Tsuguharu Foujita ( a Japanese painter residing in France ). However my style is not primarily defined by them. I'm also influenced by Sumi Painters and Japanese block printing like Hakusai.

What is your favourite art medium?

My medium of choice has evolved over the years, starting out mainly with mechanical pencil and kolinsky brushes with India Ink. But these days I mainly use Sumi brushes in tandem with soft pastels like Sennelier, although I've recently started making my own hybrid pastel that combines aspects of the soft pastel and oil stick, but using a drying oil.

What made you pursue Emulsion Sticks over soft pastels and oil pastels?

I wanted a pastel like medium that has similar aspects to Oil Pastels, minus the tendency for Oil Pastels to never dry, making the use of a fixitive agent challenging and not always reliable. This however often means relying on a less diverse variety of pigments and dyes.

What types of dyes and pigments do you use?

Because my pastels evolved out of India Ink and Sumi Ink, I generally use natural spices and coloring agents rather than more synthetic coloring agents. This includes tumeric, coffee, chocolate, coffee, and tobacco. Activated charcoal, being the coldest and darkest of these, is used more sparingly.

Do you plan to work for art galleries, or some other route?

I'm currently wanting to pursue footware, pants, and tee shirt illustration. Maybe paint skateboards, and do book illustration, rather than an art gallery. Especially now that it seems like virtually anything could be considered fine art in a gallery context, leading to absurd things like "Modern Art", which is frankly an embarassment to illustrators like myself.

What materials do you like to weave or crochet with?

I currently mainly use acrylic yarn, but I'm wanting to branch out into cotton and bamboo yarn for making shawls and ponchos. I might over time warm up to sewing as well.

Color Palette Q and A

Why did you create this color palette, isn't manga generally done in black and white?

Generally manga contests request monochromatic art, even though what they actually mean is black and white. But this poses various issues with depth perception. This color palette works around this issue by having warmer colors by closer and cooler colors by further away. And those in shadow tend to be cooler in nuetral grey.

If you had the opprotunity to create an animation from your work, would you still use the same color palette?

In fact one of the other reasons I created this is I wanted a different way to set my work apart, as anime generally has more freedom allowed to it, create a disparity between how the manga looks, and how the animation looks. Here rather than the manga being black and white, and the animation in full color, it would use the same color palette of warm and cold nuetral greys.

Wouldn't most people have a hard time seeing the difference between warm and cold greys?

It's one of those things that those casually viewing my art might have trouble seeing, but the idea is that if it's not present the image just feels off, because shadows shouldn't be the exact same color temperature as other shades of grey in a web comic. It should be slightly colder in temperature. This however poses an issue with darker scenery like night time sequences.

What is your solution for darker sequences, like traveling on the moon, or being in a cave?

Generally the overall ambient darkness will be darker than at daylight, with a distinction still present between nearer and further: nearer is warmer, further is colder. And shadows are colder on average. The main difference with something like the moon is that shadows don't come from the sun from rather the Earth's light, so it will be dimmer.

How would Gothic fiction settings be depicted?

I would probably still depict characters wearing mostly darker and cold shades of grey for pants, and maybe darker warm shades of grey for socks and tee shirts to preserve contrast. The settings will be mostly the same as my normal palette, with the main difference being an overall ambient darkness.

As a fun fact, it was only recently that Gothicness was associated with wearing on black. Gothic fiction came about as a subtler direction away from "men's horror", toward a less overt depiction of gore, and focusing on atmosphere. Gothic romance is a hybrid of gothic fiction and romance fiction. The name of Gothic coming from Gothic Architecture.

How does this change differ from other Mangaka in the US?

Typically mangaka in the US either try to stay as close to guidelines as possible by doing "monochrome" with screentone, or pursue a more revolutionary approach using full color. I seek instead to "evolve" the medium of manga style art in a more fine art direction, by using a more sparse color palette that still conveys distance and nearness. I also don't use screentone, but this may change if I go the print route.

What is your goal with pursuing this approach to manga creation?

At my age I don't delude myself into thinking I'll ever make it to Japan before retirement age, especially with my ability to focus with learning Japanese, as I already have a lot on my plate with trying to become more fluent in French, as my first language is English. So ultimately I am to inspire creators in France and Japan to forge their own path, even if that means self-publishing.

And with political events as they are now, it's seeming less likely we'll live in a world where the average person moving to Japan is possible, unless you've lucked out with a fashion designer job.
